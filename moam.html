Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) (New York City, USA)


The Museum of Modern Art (MoMA), located in New York City, is one of the world’s most influential modern art institutions. Established in 1929 by art patrons Lillie P. Bliss, Mary Quinn Sullivan, and Abby Aldrich Rockefeller, MoMA was founded with the revolutionary goal of showcasing modern art, a concept that was not widely embraced at the time. The museum began as a small space in midtown Manhattan, aiming to challenge traditional museum norms by focusing on contemporary and avant-garde works. Its first exhibition, which featured works by Cézanne, Van Gogh, Seurat, and Gauguin, set the tone for its groundbreaking approach to art curation. Over the decades, MoMA expanded both its collection and physical space, becoming a global leader in modern and contemporary art. Its collection spans a wide range of mediums, including painting, sculpture, photography, design, film, and performance art. Among its most celebrated works are Vincent van Gogh's The Starry Night, Pablo Picasso's Les Demoiselles d'Avignon, Salvador Dalí's The Persistence of Memory, and Jackson Pollock’s One: Number 31, 1950. In addition to its vast permanent collection, MoMA is renowned for its dynamic temporary exhibitions, film screenings, and educational programs, which engage millions of visitors annually. In 1939, MoMA moved to its current location on West 53rd Street, and the museum has undergone several renovations and expansions since then, most notably by architects Philip Johnson, Yoshio Taniguchi, and Diller Scofidio + Renfro. Today, MoMA is not only a repository for iconic works of modern art but also a hub for critical dialogue and innovation in contemporary art and design, firmly establishing itself as a cultural landmark in New York City and the world.
