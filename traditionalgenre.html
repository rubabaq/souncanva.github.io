Traditional Genre

The traditional art genre reflects the richness of cultural heritage, capturing timeless stories, symbols, and artistry through painting, sculpture, and calligraphy. It bridges the past and present, preserving the beauty of tradition and craft for generations to admire.

The School Of Athens

Raphael, also known as Raffaello Sanzio, created The School of Athens, a masterpiece of the High Renaissance. The fresco, painted between 1509 and 1511 in the Vatican’s Apostolic Palace, captures the essence of classical philosophy and humanism. At its center, Plato and Aristotle symbolize the balance of idealism and empiricism, reflecting the intellectual currents of the Renaissance. Raphael included contemporary figures like Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo as ancient philosophers, demonstrating his admiration and inspiration drawn from his peers. This work showcases Raphael’s mastery of perspective, harmony, and the fusion of art and intellect.

Artist: Raffaello Sanzio

The Tribute Money

The Tribute Money is a renowned fresco painted by Masaccio, a pivotal figure in the Italian Renaissance. Created around 1425 in the Brancacci Chapel in Florence, the painting depicts a biblical story from the Gospel of Matthew, where Christ instructs Peter to retrieve a coin from a fish’s mouth to pay the temple tax. Masaccio's use of linear perspective and chiaroscuro highlights his innovative approach to creating depth and realism, reflecting his inspiration from classical antiquity and humanist ideals. His work marked a significant departure from the Gothic style, laying the groundwork for Renaissance art.

Artist: Masaccio

Estimated Value: $3053

The Death Of Socrates

"The Death of Socrates" was painted by Jacques-Louis David in 1787. The artwork portrays the final moments of Socrates, who willingly accepts death by hemlock rather than renounce his principles, symbolizing the triumph of reason and virtue over tyranny. David’s neoclassical style reflects his admiration for ancient philosophy and republican ideals, inspired by Enlightenment thought and the political climate of pre-revolutionary France. The composition, with its dramatic gestures and stark clarity, emphasizes moral fortitude and the importance of intellectual integrity. David, a leading figure of Neoclassicism, sought to evoke civic virtue and inspire his contemporaries through historical themes.

Artist: Jacques-Louis David

Estimated Value: $12.9

The Third Of May 1808

"The Third of May 1808" was painted by Francisco Goya. This iconic work captures the horrors of war, depicting the execution of Spanish rebels by Napoleon's troops during the Peninsular War. Goya's use of stark lighting, raw emotion, and unflinching realism conveys the brutality and inhumanity of violence. The central figure, a Christ-like martyr with arms raised, emphasizes sacrifice and resistance. Painted in 1814, the work reflects Goya's disillusionment with war and his empathy for human suffering. As a leading Romantic artist, Goya often explored themes of political oppression, human folly, and the darker sides of human nature.

Artist: Francisco Goya

Estimated Value: $343

The Raft Of the Medusa

"The Raft of the Medusa" is painted by Théodore Géricault in 1818-1819. The painting depicts the aftermath of the wreck of the French ship Méduse, highlighting the suffering of survivors. Géricault’s thought process was deeply influenced by the tragedy, as he researched firsthand accounts and even visited survivors. His aim was to evoke human suffering and dignity in the face of disaster, showcasing realism and emotional intensity. Géricault, a Romantic artist, focused on intense, dramatic moments, and the work became a symbol of human resilience and tragedy. The painting is now housed in the Louvre Museum.

Artist: Théodore Géricault

Estimated Value: $454

Mona Lisa

The Mona Lisa was painted by Leonardo da Vinci, a Renaissance artist known for his mastery in multiple fields. The painting showcases da Vinci's exploration of human emotion and psychological depth through the subject's enigmatic smile and subtle use of light and shadow. It's believed to be a portrait of Lisa Gherardini, a Florentine woman, although its true meaning remains a mystery. Da Vinci’s careful observation of nature and the human form, combined with his understanding of anatomy, brought a level of realism and complexity to the piece. The painting has been admired for its composition and continues to spark intrigue due to its unfinished, layered quality.

Artist: Leonardo Da Vinci

Estimated Value: $860 million

Girl with a Pearl Earring

The painting Girl with a Pearl Earring was created by Johannes Vermeer, a Dutch Baroque artist. Painted around 1665, it is one of Vermeer’s most celebrated works. The painting’s subject, often referred to as the “Mona Lisa of the North,” is a young woman wearing a blue and yellow turban and a large pearl earring. The simplicity of the composition, combined with Vermeer’s mastery of light and color, creates an air of mystery around the girl’s expression. Vermeer’s use of the camera obscura, a device for projecting images, is thought to have influenced the detail and realism of his paintings.

Artist: Johannes Vermeer

Estimated Value: $3391

Arnolfini Portrait

The "Arnolfini Portrait" was created by Jan van Eyck in 1434. Van Eyck, a master of Early Netherlandish painting, is renowned for his detailed oil paintings, which bring forth the texture of objects, light, and skin. This iconic painting depicts Giovanni di Nicolao di Arnolfini and his wife in a domestic setting. The couple’s poses and the inclusion of numerous symbolic elements—such as the mirror reflecting the room’s interior—are part of van Eyck’s masterful ability to convey depth, wealth, and the intimate relationship between the two subjects. The precise rendering and the innovative use of oil paints mark a turning point in the development of Renaissance art.

Artist: Jan van Eyck

Estimated Value: $55.24
