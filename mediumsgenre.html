Mediums Genre

Mediums in art are the materials used to create artwork, such as oil paint, acrylic, watercolor, pastels, charcoal, ink, sculpture materials like stone or clay, and digital media. Each medium influences the style and impact of the piece.

Guernica

Guernica by Pablo Picasso is a powerful anti-war painting created in 1937, inspired by the bombing of the Basque town of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War. Picasso was deeply moved by the tragedy and wanted to express the horrors and suffering caused by war. The work, done in stark black and white, uses distorted, anguished figures and symbolic imagery to depict chaos, pain, and devastation. Picasso’s thought process was driven by a desire to convey the emotional and physical destruction of innocent lives, using abstraction to communicate the universality of human suffering. The piece became a global symbol of protest against violence and war.

Artist: Pablo Picasso

Estimated Value: $309

Water Lillies

Claude Monet's Water Lilies series, created during the last decades of his life, reflects his deep connection with nature. Monet was captivated by the water garden at his home in Giverny, France, using it as his muse. The series captures the tranquil beauty of the water’s surface, light, and reflections, focusing on the changing effects of time and weather. His innovative approach to capturing light and color, as well as the absence of traditional perspective, marked a shift toward abstract art. Monet's work revolutionized the Impressionist movement, creating timeless depictions of nature's fluidity.

Artist: Claude Monet

Estimated Value: $65.5 million

The Weeping Woman

The Weeping Woman (1937) by Pablo Picasso is a powerful portrayal of emotional anguish, created in the midst of the Spanish Civil War. Picasso's inspiration came from his personal response to the bombing of Guernica, where he sought to express the pain and suffering of the Spanish people, especially women, affected by violence. The distorted form of the woman with tear-streaked face represents a deeper, universal suffering. Picasso's angular and fragmented style reflects the intense emotional and psychological turmoil of the time, marking it as one of his most iconic works.

Artist: Pablo Picasso

Estimated Value: $1.6 million

Sunflowers

Vincent van Gogh’s Sunflowers (1888) was a series of still lifes that celebrated the vibrant beauty of sunflowers. Inspired by his move to Arles, France, van Gogh sought to capture the warmth and joy of nature. The series was meant to adorn the room of his friend, artist Paul Gauguin, who was visiting. Through bold, swirling brushstrokes and vivid yellow tones, van Gogh conveyed both the beauty and transience of life. The sunflowers, while radiant, also hint at decay, a reflection of van Gogh’s own struggles with mental health and his search for meaning. This iconic work is now celebrated for its emotional depth and innovation in color and texture.

Artist: Vincent van Gogh

Estimated Value: $450

The Old Guitarist

The Old Guitarist (1903-1904) is one of Picasso's most iconic works from his Blue Period, a time when he was deeply affected by personal tragedy and the poverty he experienced in Barcelona. The painting features an elderly, emaciated man with a guitar, symbolizing both vulnerability and strength. Picasso’s inspiration was drawn from his own emotional state, reflecting themes of isolation, suffering, and the search for solace in art. The blue tones evoke melancholy, while the distorted form of the guitarist demonstrates Picasso’s growing interest in abstraction. The work captures a raw, human experience, emphasizing the connection between music and emotional expression.

Artist: Picasso

Estimated Value: $149

The Night Watch

The Night Watch (1642) by Rembrandt van Rijn is one of his most famous works, showcasing a dramatic scene of a militia company preparing for action. The painting’s storytelling element lies in its vibrant portrayal of motion and personality, rather than the traditional static group portrait. Rembrandt’s innovative use of light and shadow, along with the dynamic positioning of figures, suggests a sense of immediacy and action. The piece reflects the artist’s exploration of realism and psychology, as he sought to capture not just the individuals' appearances, but their roles and character. Rembrandt, a master of the Dutch Golden Age, drew inspiration from his desire to depict both the grandeur and humanity of his subjects, marking a shift from traditional group portraits to more complex narrative-driven art.

Artist: Rembrandt van Rijn

Estimated Value: $102

Impression, Sunrise

"Impression, Sunrise" (1872) by Claude Monet is a pioneering work that sparked the Impressionist movement. Painted in the port of Le Havre at sunrise, Monet captured the fleeting light and atmospheric effects rather than focusing on detail. His goal was to convey the essence of a moment, not a precise representation. The title itself, "Impression," reflects Monet's desire to evoke an emotional response rather than an exact portrayal. This piece challenged traditional painting norms, emphasizing color and light over form, and forever changed the art world. Monet’s innovation laid the foundation for modern art.

Artist: Claude Monet

Estimated Value: $299

The Sleeping Gypsy

The artist of The Sleeping Gypsy is Henri Rousseau, a French post-impressionist painter. The painting, created in 1897, depicts a mysterious scene of a woman sleeping peacefully in a desert while a lion stands nearby, gazing at her. Rousseau was inspired by his fascination with exotic landscapes and dreams. Despite lacking formal training, his self-taught style became renowned for its vivid colors and naive yet captivating approach. The painting conveys a sense of surreal tranquility, with elements of mystery and adventure, reflecting Rousseau's dreamlike vision of the world.

Artist: Henri Rousseau

Estimated Value: $175.75
